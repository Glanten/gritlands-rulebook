Consequences

Every character starts with the following Consequence Track. This can be modified by Skills and Talents.

0 1 2 3 4 0
Healthy Minor Moderate Major Critical Terminal

Note that Consequences can be Physical or Psyche (and there might be other, more unusual types of Consequence out there too), which can be an important distinction when certain skills are interacting with them.

Consequences In Depth

The consequence track has 2 dimensions:

The first is the length of the track - the number of spaces (slots) that it contains.

The second is the intensity of each slot. These escalate as you move along the track from no consequences heading towards the negative end of the track which indicates character death.

HARM and CONSEQUENCES

As the name implies, the Consequence Track is used to monitor the consequences your character is suffering from. Before we talk about consequences, we need to understand where they come from.

Overview

  • Harm describes the things that can hurt you.
  • Harm can come from a variety of sources; close combat hits, firearms shots, radiation, the nineties, poison gas, terrifying and horrifying experiences.
  • In the Gritlands your ability to resist avoid and overcome harm comes from a variety of source, tenacity, luck, cunning and physical resilience.
  • HARM is damage inflicted on your character.
  • Whenever HARM is inflicted you may choose to spend a point of GRIT to soak the damage and ignore it.
  • If you don't spend GRIT then you take a CONSEQUENCE.
  • You can choose not to spend a point of GRIT in a TASK and take (psyche) HARM instead.
  • Some forms of Damage are GRIEVOUS HARM. You cannot spend grit to avoid consequences from GRIEVOUS HARM.
  • The Source of the HARM generally determines the Type of Consequence.
  • The characters Consequence Track determines the Severity.
  • More Severe Consequences last longer, impair your character more, and may worsen, require treatment and, ultimately kill your character dead.

Sources of HARM

The source or cause of the harm flavours its impact on the character, and its potential long term effects.

Source Type
Combat, firearms, torture Physical
Fear, stress, terror, BLITs, torture Psyche
Radiation, nanites, BLITs, poison Systemic

Physical: This covers combat damage, over exertion, sprains, breaks, impact damage and other physical trauma. This can be treated with the First aid, Medic and Surgeon skills, and a variety of chemicals and devices.

Psyche: this covers stress, anxiety and psychological harm caused by fear, terror, horror, torture, Some chemical effects and some other rare attacks. Treatment is through Counselling, Psychoanalysis, shock therapy and some chemicals and devices.

Systemic: This covers the broad spectrum damage wrought internally to organs, systems and DNA by Radiation, Poison, Gas, Nanites, Disease and Infection. This can be treated with First Aid, Medicine and a variety of Chemicals and Devices

Exotic/other: very, very occasionally there may be other forms of HARM. These will be fully briefed to players when they encounter them.

Suffering Consequences

If you do not absorb the HARM by spending GRIT (or using appropriate skills or equipment), then you suffer a consequence.

Severity

Each Consequence fills one of the slots on the Characters Consequence Track. The player is usually going to want to select the lowest open spot but if you do get shotguned in the chest at point blank range feel free to play on hard mode and go straight to Major. The Slot determines the Severity.

The severity determines the impact of the Consequence - which effects your mobility, combat ability and skill use as defined in the following table. It also effects the recovery and deterioration times.

Consequence Type Deterioration Requires medical attention
Minor Does not deteriorate Requires medical attention
Moderate Does not deteriorate 4 hours
Major 1 hour (if not stabilised) 8 hours (once stabilised)
Critical 1 hour (if not stabilised) Recovers at the end of the event (if stabilised)
Terminal Character dies at end of day (or before) Does not recover

Recovery

Minor Consequences recover unaided after 10 minutes. The space on the track is emptied and the next consequence can be assigned there.

Moderate Consequence recover unaided after 4 hours. The space on the track is emptied and the next consequence can be assigned there.

Update: Minor and Moderate Consequences do not recover without the use of the Medic skill; the roleplaying effects of Consequences may be mitigated by the First Aid skill.

Major consequences recover unaided after 8 hours, once they have been stabilised.

Critical Consequences do not recover on their own.

Deterioration

Major Consequences worsen after 1 hour (unless stabilised). When they worsen they inflict a Critical Consequences.

Critical Consequences worsen after 1 hours (unless treated). When the worsen they inflict a Terminal Consequence.

This is the end, my friend

A Terminal Consequence cannot be treated. The character dies at the end of the day, or earlier as the player decides.

Mechanical Penalties

Having your consequence slots filled makes you less good at stuff and gives the following mechanical disadvantages:

Consequence Type Skill Penalty Bead-Pull Effect
Minor N/A 1 Disadvantage
Moderate Skills and TASKS cost +1 GRIT 2 Disadvantage
Major Skills and TASKS cost +2 GRIT 3 Disadvantage
Critical No Skill use No Skill use
Terminal No Skill use No Skill use

Portraying Consequences

When taking a consequence the most important thing is to adhere to the spirit of the Limitations imposed.

Players are encouraged to put a dramatic and appropriate role-play spin on Consequences.

Rather than produce a ludicrously large table of effects like a mid 90s TTRPG here is some guidance on how you could represent the effects of some Physical CONSEQUNCES based on the location that got hit. Go forth and roleplay.

Advanced Consequence Theory

The entire point of consequences is to encourage cool, dramatic, varied injuries and reactions to the horrors of the Gritlands. To specifically avoid "I've been hit x times in a given location it stops working" or "I'm at zero I fall over".

In the vast majority of cases the Type of consequences you take should be linked to the source of the damage. But occasionally it is fine to mix this up:

  • You might choose to take a point of physical HARM as a mental consequence to represent the fact that the physical wound is not as debilitating to your character as the fear and panic that being shot causes them.
  • In different circumstances you might decide to represent the impact to Mental HARM caused by a terrifying monster through nausea rather than panic - as your body has a physiological reaction to the surge in stress hormones and adrenaline.
  • Similarly if poisoned you might take a mental or system consequence to represent a mania, or sickness inflicted by the chemicals.

Generally such swapping of consequences should be reserved for Minor and Moderate Consequences only.

Major, and Definitely Critical Consequences should follow directly from the source of HARM.

Alternative Consequence Examples

Blind: Best suited to Physical / Systemic and Chemical sources of HARM.

You go blind. You should close your eyes (opening them only to allow you to move safely if you are on uneven ground or moving at pace). You can move at any speed but are entirely unable to fight or shoot. You might still be able to use certain skills - where there is no requirement to see - and even operate some simple devices.

If a Major Blindness is not stabilised your eyes become infected and you suffer Systemic Critical Consequences - Collapsing, screaming in pain.

Reckless: Best suited to high pressure dangerous situations, and lower level Consequences (say minor and moderate)

You cannot move away from trouble (this would generally include refusing to leave the HOTZONE unless everyone else is leaving too)

You can attack, but defend poorly (you most likely discard your shield)

You can maintain your rate of fire - but waste every other shot firing into the floor/air
